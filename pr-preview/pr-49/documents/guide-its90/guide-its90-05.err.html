<title id="_9d05e926-c987-3662-b0d8-d0bc243d89ee">Typical designs of SPRTs</title>
<p id="_1b97bebf-c988-7668-b62a-e32adf73f89e">The platinum wire for the sensing elements in all SPRTs is obtained “hard drawn”, as it is easier to handle in this condition, but it is thoroughly annealed during the manufacture of the thermometer. The sensing-element winding is usually bifilar, but occasionally other low inductance configurations are used. A low inductance is important if the thermometer is to be used in ac measurement circuits, and reduces the SPRT sensitivity to electromagnetic interference. In all SPRTs, the sensing element must be supported in a strain-free manner on a structure, usually made of clean high-purity polished silica, with no rough or sharp edges. As a result, a well designed and manufactured SPRT does not show hysteresis during thermal cycling. The sensing element is sealed in a suitable atmosphere and connected within the sheath to four platinum leads, two for the passage of the current and two for sensing the voltage. This is to enable a truly four-wire resistance measurement, in which lead resistance effects are eliminated.</p>

<p id="_c1321657-86ec-a553-72a7-7a59b4d692cd">Mechanical, electrical and thermal constraints dictate that no SPRT can be used over the whole temperature range from <asciimath>13.8033 "unitsml(K)"</asciimath> to <asciimath>1234.93 "unitsml(K)"</asciimath>, and in practice three distinct types are used. ‘Capsule-type’ SPRTs are designed for operation in the temperature range from <asciimath>13.8 "unitsml(K)"</asciimath> to about <asciimath>430 "unitsml(K)"</asciimath>. ‘Long-stem’ SPRTs are used in the temperature range from <asciimath>-189.3 "unitsml(degC)"</asciimath> to <asciimath>660 "unitsml(degC)"</asciimath>. Both of these types typically have a resistance of about <asciimath>25 "unitsml(Ohm)"</asciimath> at the TPW, giving a nominal sensitivity of <asciimath>0.1 "unitsml(Ohm)"//"unitsml(K)"</asciimath>. At a measuring current of <asciimath>1 "unitsml(mA)"</asciimath>, the self-heating effect is usually in the range from <asciimath>0.2 "unitsml(mK)"</asciimath> to <asciimath>4 "unitsml(mK)"</asciimath>. ‘High-temperature’ long-stem SPRTs (HTSPRTs) are used at temperatures up to <asciimath>962 "unitsml(degC)"</asciimath>. Their resistance at the TPW is typically between <asciimath>0.2 "unitsml(Ohm)"</asciimath> and <asciimath>2.5 "unitsml(Ohm)"</asciimath> and higher measuring currents are used (see below).</p>

<title id="_672809ed-5805-4ec5-d8f5-efe72fd9b399">EXPERIMENTAL SOURCES OF UNCERTAINTY</title>
<p id="_185585e7-18db-36b9-5d9d-6b750cc32a80">This subsection provides a brief summary of the known sources of uncertainty affecting platinum resistance thermometry, see also the overview of factors influencing the resistance given in [<eref type="inline" bibitemid="Pokhodun2002" citeas="[64]"> <display-text>Pokhodun 2002</display-text> </eref>, <eref type="inline" bibitemid="MeyerRipple2006" citeas="[56]"> <display-text>Meyer and Ripple 2006</display-text> </eref>]. The budget for estimating the uncertainty of the ITS-90 realisation with SPRTs calibrated at the fixed points is presented in Subsection 6.</p>

<title id="_2a336f7f-dab8-7401-db2e-e65995ca0953">Resistance measurement</title>
<p id="_f30f59f4-67e0-da27-185e-a1bc059a494c">Resistance measurements in platinum thermometry are usually made using automatic low-frequency resistance bridges. The reading of the bridges is typically a dimensionless ratio <asciimath>ii(X) = ii(R)(ii(T)_{90})//ii(R)_"S"</asciimath>, where <asciimath>ii(R) (ii(T)_{90})</asciimath> is the resistance of the SPRT under the current conditions of measurement, and <asciimath>ii(R)_"S"</asciimath> is the resistance of a calibrated reference or standard resistor also connected to the bridge. The bridges all employ a 4-terminal-coaxial or guarded 4-terminal definition of resistance [<eref type="inline" bibitemid="Awan2011" citeas="[6]"> <display-text>Awan <em>et al.</em> 2011</display-text> </eref>] to reduce the effect of lead resistances and stray impedances. The major sources of uncertainty associated with the resistance measurements include the reference resistor value and its stability, and self heating of the SPRT due to the sensing power. There are additional minor effects associated with the connecting cables and the resistance bridge itself.</p>

<title id="_07884b5d-f0a7-303d-13bb-79ce8b66ab0c">UNCERTAINTY IN SPRT CALIBRATIONS</title>
<p id="_4a33b003-9094-2493-b9be-ad14e6a850fd">The estimation of the total uncertainty of a temperature value <asciimath>ii(T)_{90}</asciimath> realised with an SPRT (<xref target="scls_6-4"/>) starts with the estimation of the uncertainty of the calibration at the fixed points (<xref target="scls_6-1"/>). But for a realisation at any temperature, it is necessary to propagate the calibration uncertainty (<xref target="scls_6-2"/>) and consider the non-uniqueness of the scale realisation due to interpolation errors (<xref target="scls_6-3"/>). The estimation for practical measurements starts with the calibration uncertainty as basic component. It is complex due to the variety of sub-ranges with different interpolation equations, the option to use either single or multiple TPW measurements, possibly with different TPW cells, and the options for the use of different resistance bridges or standard resistors for different resistance measurements. It requires, furthermore, to consider the SPRT effects discussed in Subsection 5 because usually a separate TPW measurement cannot be performed for each single temperature determination. Since the practical application of SPRTs is outside the scope of this guide, here only the most important cases routinely met during calibration of SPRTs are covered. For cases not covered here, and for detailed explanations and derivations, see [<eref type="inline" bibitemid="White2018" citeas="[98]"> <display-text>White <em>et al.</em> 2018</display-text> </eref>]. In all cases, the necessary corrections and measuring errors are sufficiently small for estimating the uncertainty using a linear mathematical model.</p>

<p id="_65f1f7c2-91f3-c717-def0-1961346bb316">Some subsections below provide graphs giving numerical values for some of the uncertainty contributions and the total uncertainty. The values in these graphs are purely indicative, and are given only to illustrate the approximate magnitude and temperature dependence of the various contributions. Users are expected to determine uncertainties appropriate to their measurements and use these in the equations.</p>

<title id="_3a69bd0e-a1e5-5ed2-05d7-36ff8086351b">Propagation of calibration uncertainty</title>
<p id="_c7627977-129b-6a3f-b1d7-f65f8a956147">The many interpolating equations of the ITS-90 greatly complicate the propagation of uncertainty for temperatures measured using SPRTs. Complications also arise from the option to use either single or multiple TPW measurements, possibly with different TPW cells, and the options for the use of different resistance bridges or standard resistors for different resistance measurements. In principle, each combination of these possibilities results in a different equation for total uncertainty. Further, the complexity of ITS-90 is such that it is generally impractical to obtain correct expressions for total uncertainty without some supporting mathematics. This subsection covers the two cases routinely met during calibration of SPRTs: LSPRTs/HTSPRTs and CSPRTs. For cases not covered here, and for detailed explanations and derivations, see [White <em>et al</em>. 2018].</p>

<title id="_7139a02d-b670-195b-9d99-8bd72b1d39a4">Non-uniqueness due to interpolation errors</title>
<p id="_cabe5d60-2444-4ab0-7ff9-ef14f58e3bcb">Ideally, the interpolating equations of ITS-90 should accommodate the real variations in the <asciimath>ii(W) (ii(T)_{90}</asciimath>) relationship for different SPRTs. In practice, the functional form of the equations is insufficient to characterise the many complex physical effects that occur, and hence the SPRT calibration equations are subject to interpolation error. Interpolation error is responsible for non-uniqueness [<eref type="inline" bibitemid="Mangum1997" citeas="[47]"> <display-text>Mangum <em>et al.</em> 1997</display-text> </eref>]: ambiguity in the scale that arises because there is more than one equation used (Type 1 non-uniqueness), more than one type of interpolating instrument used (Type 2 non-uniqueness), or different instances of the designated interpolation instrument applied (Type 3 non-uniqueness). Over most of the SPRT sub-ranges of ITS-90, non-uniqueness is a major source of uncertainty.</p>

<p id="_96b014cb-cbec-3cde-eb24-e2491d66a757">In this subsection, we consider only Type 1 and Type 3 non-uniqueness in the SPRT sub-ranges. (Type 2 non-uniqueness contributes only between the triple point of equilibrium hydrogen (<asciimath>13.8033 "unitsml(K)"</asciimath>) and the triple point of neon (<asciimath>24.5561 "unitsml(K)"</asciimath>), where the SPRT and the interpolating gas thermometer sub-ranges overlap.) Type 1 and Type 3 non-uniqueness contribute up to <asciimath>0.5 "unitsml(mK)"</asciimath> or more to uncertainty in the temperatures interpolated between fixed points.</p>

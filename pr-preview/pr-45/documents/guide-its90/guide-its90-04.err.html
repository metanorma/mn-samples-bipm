<clause id="_883a80ba-8ead-77f7-f8e4-21bf7301d7f2" anchor="cls_6" obligation="normative">
<title id="_6d8fadf8-d2cd-1cf5-dd75-a445547e3361">Pressure measurement</title>
<p id="_06cc5a49-dc95-7282-6543-378dd2d37fd1">The pressure-measuring system is isolated from the working gas by a calibrated flexible diaphragm, which may be at room temperature or at the bulb temperature. (<xref target="fig1"/> depicts a diaphragm manometer reading <asciimath>p_{"R"}</asciimath> directly; this is for illustration only.) In practice, the pressure <asciimath>p_{"R"}</asciimath> measured at room temperature is the sum of measurements made with some absolute manometer and a differential (diaphragm) manometer. The considerable advantages of a diaphragm at the lower temperature are to some extent offset by the need for a design that is stable despite temperature cycling [<eref type="inline" bibitemid="Steur2003" citeas="[26]"> <display-text>Steur <em>et al.</em> 2003</display-text> </eref>, <eref type="inline" bibitemid="Steur2005" citeas="[28]"> <display-text>Steur <em>et al</em>. 2005</display-text> </eref>]. In the experiments with low-temperature diaphragms, the maximum pressure was limited to <asciimath>5 "unitsml(kPa)"</asciimath> (differential) and <asciimath>60 "unitsml(kPa)"</asciimath> (absolute). In the CVGT experiments described in [<eref type="inline" bibitemid="Berry1979" citeas="[4]"> <display-text>Berry 1979</display-text> </eref>, <eref type="inline" bibitemid="Kemp1986" citeas="[11]"> <display-text>Kemp <em>et al.</em> 1986/87</display-text> </eref>, <eref type="inline" bibitemid="Steur1986" citeas="[22]"> <display-text>Steur and Durieux 1986</display-text> </eref>, Astrov <em>et al.</em> <eref type="inline" bibitemid="Astrov1989" citeas="[1]"> <display-text>1989</display-text> </eref> and <eref type="inline" bibitemid="Astrov1995" citeas="[2]"> <display-text>1995</display-text> </eref>], differential diaphragm manometers having a full scale between <asciimath>400 "unitsml(Pa)"</asciimath> and <asciimath>1300 "unitsml(Pa)"</asciimath> were used at room temperature. In many of the cited ICVGT realizations, only an absolute diaphragm manometer was used, combined with a sufficiently high gas density. In order to reduce non-linearity effects as well as zero instabilities, a diaphragm is to be used only in the positive sense in most cases, i.e. without passing through zero, and by limiting pressure differences well below <asciimath>30 %</asciimath> of full scale. Pressure measurement at room temperature can be based, with high <em>absolute</em> accuracy, on a mercury manometer using optical interferometry or capacitive detection to measure differences in the heights of two mercury columns. On the other hand, pressure balances are preferred in gas thermometry for their ease of use and absence of mercury. High-quality devices can also have an <em>absolute</em> uncertainty of order a few parts per million. But for interpolating gas thermometry, the very high <em>relative</em> accuracy of pressure balances is of special interest. This means, a pressure-independent error of their effective area influences only the coefficients of the quadratic interpolation function, but not the interpolation results. The pressure sensitivity of a gas thermometer is proportional to the filling density, e.g. a sensitivity of about <asciimath>2500 "unitsml(Pa/K)"</asciimath> corresponds to a density of <asciimath>300 "unitsml(mol/m^3)"</asciimath>. Results have been reported for gas densities as small as <asciimath>44 "unitsml(mol/m^3)"</asciimath> (<asciimath>366 "unitsml(Pa/K)"</asciimath>) and as large as <asciimath>460 "unitsml(mol/m^3)"</asciimath> (<asciimath>3800 "unitsml(Pa/K)"</asciimath>). Pressure measurement resolution is typically <asciimath>0.01 "unitsml(Pa)"</asciimath> for diaphragm gauges, with uncertainties close to <asciimath>0.1 "unitsml(Pa)"</asciimath> for mercury manometers and pressure balances. For further information, see also the <em>Guide</em> Section 3 <em>Vapour-Pressure Thermometry and Pressure Measurement</em>.</p>

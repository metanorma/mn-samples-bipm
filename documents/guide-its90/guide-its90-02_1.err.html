<title id="_2492d9fe-6407-6ac8-e3d0-b7c0d6ccd28f">Effects of impurities in fixed-point samples</title>
<p id="_aa2280cd-6c53-1453-3634-b55fabc0a030">Background information on the behaviour of impurities during freezing and melting is contained in various text books, for instance in <eref type="inline" bibitemid="lewis1961" citeas="[48]"/>, <eref type="inline" bibitemid="gilman1963" citeas="[32]"/>, <eref type="inline" bibitemid="ubbelohde1965" citeas="[96]"/>, <eref type="inline" bibitemid="prince1966" citeas="[76]"/>, <eref type="inline" bibitemid="pfann1966" citeas="[74]"/>, <eref type="inline" bibitemid="brice" citeas="[13]"/>, <eref type="inline" bibitemid="hein" citeas="[38]"/>, <eref type="inline" bibitemid="chernov" citeas="[15]"/>, <eref type="inline" bibitemid="sloan1988" citeas="[83]"/>, <eref type="inline" bibitemid="kurz" citeas="[45]"/>, <eref type="inline" bibitemid="pimpinelli1998" citeas="[75]"/>, <eref type="inline" bibitemid="drapala" citeas="[20]"/>. The starting point for the thermodynamic description of phase transitions is establishing the equality of the thermodynamic potentials of the involved phases. In <link target="http://www.bipm.org/utils/common/pdf/ITS-90/Guide_ITS-90_2_1_Impurities_Appendix-1_2018.pdf">Appendix 1</link>, this approach is used to derive approximate relations between the basic crystallographic parameters and the relevant thermodynamic quantities. In this subsection, the relations from the literature that are needed to assess the uncertainty due to the influence of impurities are summarised.</p>

<title id="_cff9ffe6-2f1b-cd0a-d93f-308c2a72a7af">Solid Solution / Liquid Solution</title>
<p id="_35e5efe1-fcad-9524-7b13-2c89dac66699">When solid solutions of a fixed-point material and its impurities are formed (solid solution / liquid solution), three distinct conditions of segregation of the components can occur during freezing (diffusion in the solid phase can usually be neglected) <eref type="inline" bibitemid="gilman1963" citeas="[32]"/>: Complete mixing, partial mixing, and no mixing in the liquid. In the latter two cases, non-equilibrium effects have to be considered. In practice, several non-equilibrium effects affect the distribution of impurities. These include diffusion, convection, and the non-uniform advancement of the interface due to the formation of particular crystalline structures <eref type="inline" bibitemid="sloan1988" citeas="[83]"/>.</p>

<p id="_a7a4606d-ab0c-ec1f-1c28-52a8273d0424">For fixed-point realisations, diffusion effects are particularly significant. The time taken for impurities to fully diffuse and equilibrate over distances of many millimetres in the liquid phase is usually tens of hours. Consequently, the shape and temperature range of freezing plateaus can depart significantly from the equilibrium curves. The effects of convection are insignificant because of the very small temperature gradients in the sample volume. Considering the typical order of magnitude of the diffusion coefficients <asciimath>ii(D)</asciimath> in liquid metals (<asciimath>10^{-5} "unitsml(cm/s)"</asciimath>), the case of complete mixing is approximated only at very small rates of freezing, i.e., at very low velocities <asciimath>v</asciimath> of the liquid/solid interface (<asciimath>vl // ii(D) &lt; &lt; 1</asciimath>, where <asciimath>l</asciimath> is the length of the sample in the freezing direction, i.e., the direction of solid growth). Thus, for the experimental conditions normally realized, the results must be analysed carefully to determine whether the segregation of the impurities is in accordance with the strongest possible dependence given by <xref target="eq5"/> below.</p>

<title id="_3ded3c92-72fe-96ec-a59f-8006cf434d0b">Methods for estimating the effects of impurities and uncertainties</title>
<p id="_0dfcb128-a312-6cb8-533b-2ddcab9664ff">Three methods of differing significance were proposed in <eref type="inline" bibitemid="fellmuth2001" citeas="[26]"/>, <eref type="inline" bibitemid="fellmuth2005" citeas="[28]"/> with a view to obtaining a reliable estimate of the uncertainty component due to impurities. These methods are the “Sum of Individual Estimates” (SIE), the “Overall Maximum Estimate” (OME), and the “Estimate based on Representative Comparisons” (ERC). They are discussed here in turn (ERC in <xref target="cls_7"/>), and also some additional combined approaches. Since the estimates are valid for the liquidus point, where only an infinitesimal portion of the sample is frozen, the determination of the liquidus-point temperature is treated at the end of this section.</p>

<title id="_3d000491-fc9b-9706-36f1-35b4f0b4d127">Overview of effects of impurities in the ITS-90 fixed-point substances</title>
<p id="_2b1154db-7cc7-0688-654d-6f2f5e587042">The influence of impurities on the fixed-point temperature differs substantially among the three types of fixed-point substances used in the ITS-90, namely cryogenic gases, water, and metals. The differences are manifest in the number and kind of common and effectively-acting impurities, their solubility in the melt and the solid, and in the slopes of the liquidus lines. The product of the fixed-point temperature <asciimath>ii(T)_{90}</asciimath> and the cryoscopic constant <asciimath>ii(A)</asciimath> is only weakly temperature dependent (the values range from 0.9 to 2.7), see <xref target="table1"/>. This suggests that, for all substances, the relative change of the realised temperature by impurities has the same order of magnitude for a given purity. The values <asciimath>m_1^i</asciimath> of the liquidus-line slopes listed in <link target="http://www.bipm.org/utils/common/pdf/ITS-90/Guide_ITS-90_2_1_Impurities_Appendix-2_2018.pdf">Appendix 2</link>support this tendency. On average, the absolute influence of impurities is more than one order of magnitude larger for metals than for the cryogenic gases, and for water it is in between. The distribution coefficients <asciimath>k_0^i</asciimath> are tabulated in <link target="http://www.bipm.org/utils/common/pdf/ITS-90/Guide_ITS-90_2_1_Impurities_Appendix-2_2018.pdf">Appendix 2</link>for two reasons. First, they show how purification can be achieved by zone refining. Second, for many systems, <asciimath>m_1^i</asciimath> can be estimated from <asciimath>k_0^i</asciimath> by applying <xref target="eq6"/>.</p>

<title id="_dc954e84-39af-6801-7ed1-58f1edb5fc9c">Fixed-point cell assembly</title>
<p id="_a34e4793-114a-ac07-7ae1-191e97f2c98f">For calibration of a standard platinum resistance thermometer to the highest accuracy, all metal ingots should be nominally better than <asciimath>99.9999 %</asciimath> pure. Freezing point temperatures below <asciimath>420 "unitsml(degC)"</asciimath> should then be within a few tenths of a millikelvin of that of an ideally pure ingot. Zone-refined Sn and Zn with a purity of <asciimath>99.999 %</asciimath> will usually allow calibration to within <asciimath>1 "unitsml(mK)"</asciimath>, but this depends upon the particular impurities present, but see <eref type="inline" bibitemid="Connolly1980" citeas="[15]"> <display-text>Connolly and McAllan (1980)</display-text> </eref> for a discussion of this question. With Al and Ag (also Au and Cu) a purity of <asciimath>99.999 %</asciimath> will usually result in an error of a few millikelvins. The effect of impurities on the liquidus temperature is discussed in detail in  <em>Guide</em> Section 2.1 <em>Influence of impurities</em>.</p>

<p id="_5a9abdcf-da29-5645-9ebd-9cae9b6860c3">For most metal fixed points, the ingot is contained in a cylindrical graphite crucible of high purity (greater than <asciimath>99.999 %</asciimath>) and high density (greater than <asciimath>1.9 "unitsml(g)" "unitsml(cm^-3)"</asciimath>). For mercury, however, a cell of stainless steel (Type AISI 304) or borosilicate glass is used, while for gallium a flexible container of polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) or Teflon is generally preferred, to accommodate the expansion which occurs on freezing. Some success has been reported with stainless steel cells for In, Sn, and Al fixed points [<eref type="inline" bibitemid="Li2004" citeas="[30]"> <display-text>Li <em>et al.</em> 2004</display-text> </eref>].</p>

<title>Primary standards and transducers for pressure measurements</title>
<p id="_206f1544-ea7d-c9c0-b829-28e032f71f70">Pressure measurements are required for the realization of the helium vapour-pressure scales, the boiling points of hydrogen and the interpolating gas thermometer, see ITS-90 Guide Chapter 4 <em>Gas Thermometry</em>. <xref target="tab1"/> summarises helium and hydrogen vapour-pressure data and allows themeasurement requirements to be calculated. It shows that in order to cover the complete range for <sup>4</sup>He, it is necessary to measure absolute pressures from <asciimath>100 "unitsml(Pa)"</asciimath> to <asciimath>200 "unitsml(kPa)"</asciimath>, with standard uncertainties of <asciimath>0.1 "unitsml(Pa)"</asciimath> to <asciimath>15 "unitsml(Pa)"</asciimath> (relative <asciimath>0.1 %</asciimath> to <asciimath>75 "unitsml(ppm)"</asciimath>, <asciimath>"unitsml(ppm)"</asciimath> means parts per million), respectively, to achieve <asciimath>0.1 "unitsml(mK)"</asciimath> uncertainty in <asciimath>ii(T)_{90}</asciimath>. The needed relative uncertainties are less wide ranging, varying from <asciimath>30 "unitsml(ppm)"</asciimath> for hydrogen at <asciimath>20.3 "unitsml(K)"</asciimath> to <asciimath>0.1 %</asciimath> for <sup>3</sup>He at <asciimath>0.65 "unitsml(K)"</asciimath>. For gas thermometry, the range is <asciimath>30 "unitsml(ppm)"</asciimath> at <asciimath>3 "unitsml(K)"</asciimath> to <asciimath>4 "unitsml(ppm)"</asciimath> at <asciimath>25 "unitsml(K)"</asciimath>. A measuring instrument with a constant relative uncertainty is thus more suitable than one with a constant absolute uncertainty.</p>

<p id="_18f81c62-8c0d-c04c-624f-a87d945339fb">Compared with the high-level realizations of the pressure scale, the requirements (in terms of room temperature capability for pressure measurement) of vapour-pressure thermometry are not overly stringent. In the pressure range of interest here, the primary pressure standards of the national metrological institutes are based on liquid-column manometers and pressure balances.

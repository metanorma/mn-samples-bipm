<clause id="_carriers" obligation="normative">
<title>Carriers</title>
<p id="_a7d978bc-9484-7116-e167-f743c02b6199">As a general rule, the molar concentrations of the radionuclides in solution are very low. For instance, an activity concentration of <asciimath>800 "unitsml(kBq/g)"</asciimath> is equivalent to a concentration of ~ 10<sup>-8</sup> M of <sup>54</sup>Mn [<asciimath>ii(T)_(1//2) = (312.13 +- 0.03)</asciimath> days] and a concentration of ~ 10<sup>-7</sup> M of <sup>133</sup>Ba [<asciimath>ii(T)_(1//2) = (10.540 +- 0.006)</asciimath> years].</p>

<p id="_62655fc6-2bed-509f-bfd3-858db88b4133">The solutions are very dilute and adsorption of radioactive ions on the surfaces of containers can be substantial. Adding a carrier makes it possible to reduce the concentration losses due to adsorption on the container walls and thus keep the radioactive ion in solution.</p>

000990cleaning Hanging paragraph in clause
<clause id="cleaning" obligation="normative">
<title>Cleaning and packaging of glass ampoules</title>
<p id="_8891250e-b870-0a60-6422-268c453a55cb">The process of deterioration of the glass surface can occur in the presence of the solution and of air (<eref type="inline" bibitemid="adams2" citeas="Adams, 1972"/>) as is schematically shown in <xref target="fig28"/>. The phenomena which can occur in sequence or simultaneously are the following: water adsorption, ion exchange, salt formation, leaching, etching, deposit forming, hydration and dehydration.</p>

<p id="_e9ca48fb-2cb4-faee-eb8b-aa43632788b0">As previously mentioned in <xref target="mechanisms"/>, the glass surface is not smooth on a microscale. All glass is porous at the <asciimath>0.1 "unitsml(nm)"</asciimath> level (<eref type="inline" bibitemid="adams2" citeas="Adams, 1972"/>). The porosity of glass that has been leached to selectively remove certain elements from the surface can increase to a depth of <asciimath>1 "unitsml(nm)"</asciimath> to <asciimath>10 "unitsml(nm)"</asciimath> (<eref type="inline" bibitemid="adams2" citeas="Adams, 1972"/>). Yet, increasing the porosity of the surface glass results in an increase in the number of adsorption sites. Therefore, the phenomenon of sorption of radioactive ions can occur when the solution is stored in sealed-glass ampoules.</p>
